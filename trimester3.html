Information for Trimester 3

Here’s a comprehensive information package for women in their third trimester of pregnancy (weeks 29-40). The final trimester is an exciting time, but it can also bring new challenges as the body prepares for labor and the baby continues to grow rapidly. 1. What to Expect in the Third Trimester Physical Changes: The baby bump is now much larger, and the baby may “drop” lower into the pelvis toward the end. Braxton Hicks contractions may become more frequent and stronger. Breast changes continue, and you may leak more colostrum. Increased pressure on the bladder can lead to more frequent urination. Shortness of breath is common due to the baby pushing against the diaphragm. Weight Gain: Expect to gain about 0.5 to 1 pound per week, though this can vary. Fetal Movements: You should feel regular kicks, rolls, and movements. Report any significant decrease in activity to your doctor. 2. Nutrition and Hydration Increased Nutritional Needs: The baby is rapidly gaining weight, so focus on nutrient-rich foods. Calcium and Protein: Continue eating calcium-rich foods (dairy, leafy greens) and protein (lean meats, eggs, beans) to support the baby’s growth. Iron: Keep up with iron-rich foods (red meat, spinach, legumes) to prevent anemia and support blood flow. Fiber: As constipation can worsen, eat high-fiber foods like fruits, vegetables, and whole grains to maintain bowel regularity. Hydration: Staying hydrated helps manage swelling and reduces the risk of urinary tract infections. Aim for at least 8-10 glasses of water per day. 3. Medical Checkups and Monitoring Prenatal Visits: Visits increase in frequency, usually every 2 weeks from weeks 28-36, then weekly from 36 weeks until delivery. Doctors will monitor: Blood pressure (watching for signs of preeclampsia) Baby’s position (head-down, breech, etc.) Fetal heart rate and movement Fundal height (measuring the growth of the uterus) Symptoms like swelling, headaches, or visual changes. Group B Streptococcus (GBS) Test: This test is typically done between weeks 35-37. GBS is a common bacterium that can cause infection in newborns, and if you test positive, you’ll receive antibiotics during labor. Fetal Non-Stress Test (NST): May be conducted in the final weeks to monitor the baby’s heart rate in response to movement. 4. Preparing for Labor and Delivery Signs of Labor: Contractions: Regular, increasingly intense contractions that don't go away with movement. Water Breaking: A sudden gush or steady trickle of amniotic fluid. Bloody Show: Passage of a mucus plug tinged with blood, signaling that labor may be near. Braxton Hicks vs. True Labor: Braxton Hicks: Irregular and usually painless contractions that don’t increase in intensity. True Labor: Regular contractions that become more intense over time and do not go away with changes in position or activity. Birth Plan: Finalize your birth plan, including preferences for pain relief (epidural, natural methods), labor positions, and who will be in the delivery room. However, remain flexible as situations can change. Hospital Bag: Prepare a hospital bag around 36 weeks. Include essentials for yourself, your partner, and the baby (clothes, toiletries, baby clothes, diapers, important documents). 5. Exercise and Movement Stay Active: Continue with light to moderate exercise, like walking or swimming, to reduce discomfort and help prepare the body for labor. Pelvic Floor Exercises: Continue with Kegel exercises to strengthen the pelvic muscles, which can aid in delivery and recovery. Labor Preparation: Some women may begin exercises like prenatal yoga or squats to prepare the body for delivery. Avoid Heavy Lifting: Be mindful of your body’s limits and avoid lifting heavy objects to reduce the risk of injury. 6. Common Discomforts Swelling (Edema): Swelling in the feet and ankles is common. Elevate your feet when sitting, drink plenty of water, and avoid standing for long periods. Heartburn: Continue eating smaller meals and avoid spicy or greasy foods. Sitting upright after meals and sleeping with your head elevated can help. Back Pain: As the belly grows, the lower back takes more strain. Use a belly band for support and try prenatal massages or warm baths to relieve discomfort. Fatigue: Rest as needed, especially if sleep is disrupted by discomfort. Use pillows to support the belly and legs when sleeping on your side. Frequent Urination: The baby’s position may put extra pressure on the bladder. Continue to stay hydrated, but try reducing fluid intake before bedtime to reduce nighttime trips to the bathroom. 7. Baby’s Development Weeks 29-32: The baby’s brain and nervous system are rapidly developing, and it can open and close its eyes. Weeks 33-36: The baby’s bones harden, but the skull remains flexible for birth. The lungs are nearly fully developed, and the baby continues to gain weight. Weeks 37-40: The baby is now considered full-term at 37 weeks. It gains about half a pound per week and may drop lower into the pelvis in preparation for birth. 8. Mental and Emotional Well-being Anxiety and Excitement: It's normal to feel a mix of excitement and anxiety as the due date approaches. Talk to your partner, family, or friends for emotional support. Birth Plan Flexibility: Labor and delivery can be unpredictable, so while having a plan is important, being adaptable can help manage any changes or surprises. Bonding with Baby: Continue bonding with your baby by talking, reading, or singing. Partners can also get involved by engaging with the baby through touch and sound. 9. Preparing for Postpartum Breastfeeding Preparation: If planning to breastfeed, consider attending a breastfeeding class or consulting a lactation specialist. Postpartum Supplies: Stock up on postpartum essentials like maternity pads, comfortable clothing, nursing bras, and nipple cream. Emotional Support: Postpartum can be an emotional time. Plan for support from your partner, family, or a postpartum doula to help with the transition to parenthood. 10. Signs of Labor and When to Call the Doctor Contractions: If contractions are regular and increasing in intensity (coming every 5 minutes for more than an hour), call your healthcare provider. Water Breaking: If your water breaks (a gush or steady trickle of fluid), contact your doctor immediately, even if contractions haven’t started. Reduced Fetal Movement: If you notice a significant decrease in fetal movement, contact your doctor. You should feel at least 10 movements in 2 hours. Severe Symptoms: Call your doctor if you experience: Severe headaches, blurred vision, or sudden swelling (could be signs of preeclampsia). Vaginal bleeding or fluid leakage. Severe abdominal pain or pressure. 11. Partner Involvement Encourage your partner to attend prenatal visits, especially as the due date approaches. Discuss how your partner can support you during labor and delivery (helping with breathing techniques, providing emotional support, etc.). Partners can also help prepare the home for the baby, participate in prenatal classes, and bond with the baby by talking or singing to the belly. This third-trimester guide covers critical aspects of health, preparation for labor, and self-care. Every pregnancy is unique, so regular consultations with healthcare providers are essential for personalized advice as delivery approaches.
